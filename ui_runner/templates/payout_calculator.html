🎃 How Goblin Lines Work

Goblins move the line in your favor (lower for OVER props). You must pick MORE on Goblins. They reduce the payout multiplier because they're easier to hit. The further the line moves from standard, the bigger the payout reduction. On a mixed slip, each Goblin leg pulls the overall multiplier down.

Example: LeBron standard points = 24.5. Goblin = 19.5 (5 units easier).
A 3-leg all-standard Power Play pays 6x. The same slip with this Goblin might pay ~4.2x instead.

Key insight: Goblins are not always bad value. If the reduced line is significantly easier to hit and the payout only drops modestly, it can still be +EV.
😈 How Demon Lines Work

Demons move the line against you (higher for OVER props). You must pick MORE on Demons. They increase the payout multiplier because they're harder to hit. Demon boosts vary widely — a +2 unit move might give a small bump while a +10 unit move can push toward the 2000x maximum.

Example: LeBron standard points = 24.5. Demon = 29.5 (5 units harder).
A 3-leg all-standard Power Play pays 6x. The same slip with this Demon might pay ~9–12x depending on how far the line moved.

Key insight: Demon boosts are often not proportional to the actual difficulty increase. PrizePicks captures extra margin here — log your observations to see if the boost is worth the harder line.
🧮 Mixed Slip Payout Logic
PrizePicks does NOT publish the formula for how Goblin/Demon legs adjust the slip multiplier. However, based on community observations, the mechanics appear to work roughly like this:

1. Base multiplier = standard payout for the leg count (e.g. 6x for 3 legs)
2. Per-leg adjustment = each Goblin/Demon applies a multiplier factor to the base
3. Goblin factor ≈ reduces payout (factor < 1.0, e.g. 0.65–0.85 per leg)
4. Demon factor ≈ increases payout (factor > 1.0, e.g. 1.3–2.5+ per leg depending on distance)
5. Factors multiply together — one Goblin and one Demon in the same slip partially offset each other

The exact factors are dynamic per-prop and are not disclosed. Logging real observations is the only way to reverse-engineer them.
